The Poisoned Schoolhouse

“The health department said the three schools — Starms Early Childhood, Fernwood Montessori and LaFollette — ‘pose an immediate and ongoing risk’ due to dangerous lead hazards.”
— WPR, May 2025

“What occurred was the deliberate deferral of Black humanity. Milwaukee did not simply fail its Black children; the state did what it was built to do—manage Black existence, diminish Black life chances, and authorize slow, state-approved death under the deceitful banner of public education.”

— Ebony Rose, "Necropolitical Waters"

The 2024-2025 Milwaukee Public Schools lead crisis is not a story of accident or nature. It is the product of neglect, anti-Blackness, and bureaucratic abandonment—where schools, built to nurture, became sites of sanctioned slow death.

This interactive report lays bare the chronology, theory, and human cost behind the phrase “predictable disaster.”

Key Responses and Accountability Measures (Summary)

The unfolding crisis prompted a series of actions and highlighted areas of responsibility across various levels:

  • Milwaukee Public Schools (MPS): Faced intense scrutiny, leading to the departure of its Senior Director of Facilities and Maintenance (Sean Kane, April 2025). MPS released a "Lead Action Plan" (April 28, 2025), initiated school closures for remediation, and began exploring legal action against lead paint manufacturers.
  • Milwaukee Health Department (MHD): Conducted initial school risk assessments, issued public health warnings, provided free lead testing clinics for students, and maintained a public dashboard of investigation statuses.
  • State of Wisconsin (DHS & Governor): The Department of Health Services (DHS) issued a noncompliance order to MPS for unsafe work practices (March 19, 2025). Governor Evers proposed over $300 million for statewide lead abatement in the 2025-27 budget.
  • Federal Government (Trump Administration): In a critical policy shift, the CDC's dedicated lead exposure prevention team was disbanded (April 1, 2025), and Milwaukee's request for on-site CDC expertise was subsequently denied due to these staff cuts. No direct federal emergency funding or EPA intervention for the MPS crisis was reported.

Further details on accountability are woven throughout the original research paper.

Lead Level Severity (Conceptual)

Reported levels up to 170x safety threshold.

Lead is highly toxic, especially to children under 6. Even low-level exposure can cause irreversible harm to the developing brain and nervous system.

  • Cognitive Impairment: Significant reduction in IQ, poor school performance.
  • Developmental Delays: Late milestones in speech, language, and attention.
  • Learning & Memory Problems: Difficulty concentrating, processing information.
  • Behavioral Issues: Impulsivity, aggression, hyperactivity.

Levels like "170x the safety threshold" indicate a severe and immediate hazard, requiring urgent action to prevent further exposure. There is no safe level of lead in a child's blood. (Source: CDC, "Health and Developmental Consequences of Lead Exposure in Children" section of this report)

Deeper Analysis: Towards a New Humanitas

The Milwaukee crisis is more than a policy failure; it reflects a fundamental devaluation of Black humanity. This section delves into Sylvia Wynter's critique of the dominant Western concept of "Man" and its role in such crises, and presents the report's call for a radical reimagining of public responsibility and human value.

Wynter's Critique of "Man" and Its Others

Sylvia Wynter argues that the Western concept of "Man" is defined by a violent exclusivity—an "overrepresentation" that structures modernity and necessitates the abjection of Blackness. The abandonment of Milwaukee’s Black children is a predictable outcome of this order, where their lives are coded as less worthy, their health and futures not afforded the same sacrosanct status. The crisis is a material manifestation of the "coloniality of being," where systemic devaluation of Black life permits such slow violence.

“If, at the deepest, ontological level, these children had been perceived as fully “Man”—as embodying the same inherent human worth... as those residing in affluent, blindingly white communities, would this utterly catastrophic failure of care... have been tolerated for even a single day, let alone for decades? Wynter’s work compels us—it forces us—to answer with a resounding, heart-wrenching, painful: likely not.”

— Ebony Rose, "Necropolitical Waters"

The Call for a New "Science of the Word" & Humanitas as Praxis

Wynter’s project, however, is one of radical possibility. She calls for a new "science of the Word"—a decolonial praxis to unsettle existing "genres of the human" and forge a world beyond the overrepresentation of "Man." This requires centering the knowledges and experiences of the historically excluded. The Milwaukee crisis is an urgent impetus to confront the codes rendering Black children vulnerable. "Humanitas as praxis" means collectively generating new, inclusive narratives of what it means to be human, recognizing the equal value of all. This is the hope Wynter’s work offers: to move "after Man, towards the Human," ensuring no child is sacrificed to exclusionary humanism.

“Reclaiming Black futures, then, is not merely about remediating lead in school buildings... It is about remediating the toxic epistemologies, the poisonous ‘descriptive statements,’ that allow such conditions to exist... The fight for safe schools in Milwaukee... is intrinsically linked to the broader, planetary struggle for Black liberation and the radical redefinition of what it means to be human.”

— Ebony Rose, "Necropolitical Waters" (Conclusion)
